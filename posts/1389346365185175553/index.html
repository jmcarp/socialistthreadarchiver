Charlottesville City Council
here's the agenda for tonight's charlottesville city council meeting. the full packet is online here: https://t.co/4Z4KTdIcIp https://t.co/p3X0Qd0c9H
putting the proclamations in the agenda is a great new addition. maybe for bike month they can actually do something about the extremely unsafe bike lanes? https://t.co/z4N0fPQeXZ
dr. denise bonds from the blue ridge health district is here tonight giving another pandemic update, focusing mainly on the vaccine rollout. cases are pretty low in charlottesville now, thankfully. https://t.co/LLw8ixbl4V
this per capita data by race is based on statewide (not regional or city) data. black & hispanic people have more covid infections & worse outcomes. black & asian virginians are, per capita, getting vaccinated less than white virginians. https://t.co/BXiXRZsXWc
i'd be interested to hear more about this particular graph - were there particular outbreaks among hispanic virginians that contributed to those SIGNIFICANTLY higher fatality rates early last year? https://t.co/yyEsOAf3VV
please get your shots. https://t.co/fMTDljTNpQ
albemarle county, which surrounds the city of charlottesville, is #1 in the state for vaccination rate!!! we're #5! https://t.co/tXvRnvabAJ
you can get COVID vaccines at the abandoned JCPenney! you can make an appointment or just walk in! there are spanish speaking staff on mondays! you can go to https://t.co/bGiRcNMixU to find even more vaccination sites. https://t.co/suYLzI5Lm2
meetings are the enemy of dogs https://t.co/BoKa0yOuEb
dr bonds says they anticipate FDA emergency use authorization for use of the vaccine in teens under 16 sometime this summer. the health department WILL vaccinate 16 & 17 year olds now with a parent present.
the health dept is in the process of acquiring a vehicle that will serve as a mobile vaccination clinic, allowing them to target specific areas with low vaccination rates, set up mobile vax clinics at churches, etc.
big night for proclamations https://t.co/JtXQAebWZB
today is officially joy johnson day! it's wonderful to honor someone who is still alive & well and changing this community for the better. https://t.co/f86L2QJnxb
a heavy consent agenda tonight, with item 10 being kind of a Big Deal around here https://t.co/Y9L4mbeMHG
members of the 2016 blue ribbon commission issued this statement yesterday, urging council to take appropriate action on the statues immediately https://t.co/QhsV4txlOw
there is some public comment on the consent agenda. former city councilor kristin szakos says the recent state supreme court decision allows council to remove the statues immediately, but she respects their decision to follow the process outlined by the state law.
"our moral toxic waste cannot be exported to do its damage elsewhere," szakos says. we can't just MOVE these statues to be displayed elsewhere. the SCOVA decision reversed the attorneys fees awarded to the monument fund - the city should use that money to remove the statues ASAP.
szakos says "speed is of the essence," and urges council to vote to remove the statues immediately following the public hearing they are about to set for next month. "i urge you to remove them, whether or not their final disposition has been determined."
"this is really a scar," hawkins says of charlottesville's confederate statues. "an abatement has to occur." "they're an errant framing of history, one that i have to teach against."
next is UVA professor larycia hawkins, also about the statues. she traces her own lineage to people held in slavery here in virginia. "this place is pocked with memories and histories," she says. these statues are, to her, "akin to superfund sites."
cali gaston says these statues do great damage to the community by occupying public space with the message that not all people count. "it's not good for charlottesville on any level," she says, speaking as a downtown business owner.
"they are more than just symbols - we've seen that they serve as motivations for hate," gathers says of the confederate statues that continue to serve as lightning rods for hate. even 3.5 years after they sparked a nazi rally, they still draw hate group members to them.
don gathers, a co-signer on the statement from the blue ribbon commission, says the BRC did their jobs & now it's time for council to do theirs - the statues need to be removed and until they can be removed, they need to be covered.
lena seville has also called in to stress that just moving the statues out of *our* town square isn't enough - we need to ensure they aren't just moved to some other public square where they'll do the same harm. she suggests melting them down & transforming them.
adrienne dent reiterates the same talking points as previous callers: -get rid of them now -don't just put them in some other public place -maybe just melt the lee statue
ok that concludes the public comment on the consent agenda. nothing from council in response to what was shared by the community. mayor walker asks if item 8 can be pulled for a separate vote. https://t.co/5lAqyt3RnB
city manager chip boyles says council can hold the public hearing about the statue removal at their june 7 meeting and the statues can be removed 30 days after that hearing.
the consent agenda, minus item 8 (a budget amendment for CDBG allocations) passes unanimously. item 8 passes 4-1 with mayor walker dissenting.
boyles says they still do not have the full guidance from the us treasury on the rules and regulations governing how they can spend the american rescue plan $. the first payment comes in less than 2 weeks, so those rules should be coming soon.
boyles says 64 people applied for the position of executive director of the police civilian review board. the posting has closed, so they'll be making a hire from that pool.
on to public comment. leonard schoppa has concerns that the draft land use plan is asking some neighborhoods to take on the burden of increased density. it sounds like he supports density... just not in certain neighborhoods, such as his.
maddy green from the charlottesville low income housing coalition says modest solutions like adding ADUs aren't enough - there needs to be mandatory inclusive zoning & upzoning of previously exclusive neighborhoods.
jennifer koch is a consultant on the cville plans together project working on the land use map. there are some community engagement sessions coming up - you can see those & register to attend on the website https://t.co/iLC0uIJvy2
stacy miller has concerns about the proposed 21 unit development 915 6th st (on the agenda tonight for a critical slope waiver). she makes a vague allusion to concerns about affordable housing, but doesn't elaborate on them.
kate fraleigh, speaking for the people's coalition, urges council to pass a new ordinance for the police civilian review board with all the powers authorized by recent state legislation including the ability to perform independent investigations & subpoena power
peter krebs thanks council for the proclamation about bike month & says the piedmont environmental council has an event later this week called 'cycling for sustainable cities' https://t.co/jdwlrChqiV
ms rosia says she wants to talk to council about the 'gang violence,' referring to the shocking number of shots fired incidents this year. she says she is 'a former gang leader' herself in her youth and warns that more violence is coming.
on the subject of the shootings, mayor walker says one thing that goes unaddressed in these conversations is "what IS here for our youth?" creating opportunities for young people, providing safe & fulfilling extracurriculars can make a big difference.
on the subject of land use & affordable housing, councilor michael payne says density is necessary but not sufficient. we need to explore community wealth building strategies, keep pushing for land trusts
(having spent the last several years defending the city in a lawsuit based on a decision a previous council made to remove the statues, i can understand why she is choosing the most conservative course of action here but i'm not sure other attorneys would agree this is REQUIRED)
city atty lisa robertson says 'while the supreme court decision contained some favorable language,' it was based on the law before it changed in the last GA session. her advice as the city's atty is that the city should follow the procedure laid out by the new law.
on the subject of the statues - people have been asking council WHY they are choosing to follow the slower removal process laid out by the state law when the supreme court decision appears to mean we do not have to
and we are BACK and we are having a public hearing about issuing $18mil in BONDS https://t.co/ArdV8hpBPl
look, we're not bragging or anything, but the city of charlottesville is one of only 12 localities in the commonwealth with a triple A bond rating, so that's neat right?
shockingly, NO ONE wanted to make a public comment about the bond issuance. councilor snook has a question about interest rates. krisy hammill says they expect a rate of about 1.75%. the bond issuance carries 5-0.
no takers for comment during the public hearing on the community development block grant annual action plan, either. it passes unanimously with just one question from sena magill about organizational capacity, which was answered satisfactorily by erin atak, the grants coordinator
now an SUP for 64 university way. during the planning commission hearing, the property owners with concerns about the project all expressed concerns that amounted to "i don't like living near students." (note that it's surrounded by frat houses & a massive dorm complex) https://t.co/Rs9hM7607L
that seems like a really silly oversight & a simple fix! students who live .5 miles from the university & use the university bus to get around do not all need a parking space or a car at all & the code shouldn't require us to pretend they do.
this definitely can't get changed tonight, but one thing that came up in the PC hearing about this SUP is that the city code allows for a reduction in parking requirements if a development is near a CITY bus stop... but no such allowance exists for university transit.
ok that issue didn't even come up, perhaps another time. the SUP for 64 university way carried 5-0. on to the critical slope waiver for 915 6th st SE. staff & the planning commission recommended approval.
on to the last agenda item - appointment of the city attorney. council interviewed candidates for the position last week. city manager chip boyles says based on the interviews & his own research, he recommends council appoint current acting city attorney lisa robertson.
on the critical slope waiver, mayor walker voted no. vice mayor sena magill asks if she can abstain, then reluctantly votes yes. michael payne votes no, heather & lloyd vote yes. it carries 3-2.
the appointment of acting city attorney lisa robertson as city attorney carries 5-0. i guess winning a state supreme court appeal is a pretty good audition for the role. glad to see her get the title for the job she's been doing.
mayor walker (running for re-election) and heather hill (not running, term up this year) are the current reps to that working group. mayor walker said she would continue as the rep to that working group. no one acknowledged heather's hint that it's an election year.
there was an awkward several minute interlude where heather hill tried to explain that the school board wants the councilor on the school reconfiguration working group to be someone currently council who will still be on council next year
a motion was made to amend the agenda to add: appropriating $1100 from the council strategic initiatives fund to the juneteeth celebration. it carries 5-0.
we're back to matters by the public to close things out. dr larycia hawkins says she moved here not long ago from chicago to teach at UVA & the cost of living here is as high or higher than chicago.
"it paints a picture of two charlottesvilles," hawkins says - housing for students was approved "in the blink of an eye," but affordable housing for people outside the university community is such an uphill battle.
don gathers says he "wholeheartedly applaud[s] ms robertson's efforts" in the statue case, but he laments the lack of community involvement in the hiring process for the city attorney position.
ms rosia, who identifies herself as a victim of the summer of hate, asks council to consider the needs of those victims, urging them to bring the statues down as quickly as possible. (the longer this process drags on, the more likely we are to see more violence because of them)
there was a lot of passive aggressive monologuing i didn't feel compelled to capture but the meeting has ended, goodnight.
